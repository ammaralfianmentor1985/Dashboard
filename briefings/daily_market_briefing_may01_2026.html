Simple Rule to Protect Yourself — Stop Losses Are Your Seatbelt

A stop loss is an automatic instruction that closes your trade if it goes wrong — before the damage gets too big. Think of it exactly like a car seatbelt: you never plan to crash, but you always buckle up. A 2% risk per trade means if you have $1,000 in your account, you are willing to lose no more than $20 on a single trade. That keeps you in the game long enough to be right more often than you are wrong. Never trade without a stop loss set.

Why Risk Management Matters Most at All-Time Highs

Here is a truth that Wall Street rarely says out loud: the higher markets climb, the further they can fall. When the S&P 500 is at an all-time high, every piece of bad news hits harder — because there is less cushion below. Right now, three risks could cause a sharp reversal with very little warning: an escalation in the Iran conflict that fully blocks the Strait of Hormuz (which would send oil above $130 and spike inflation); a surprise miss on the May 8 jobs report that signals the economy is slowing; or a return of US tariffs in July that reignites trade war fears. None of these are predictions — they are possibilities. The correct response to all-time highs is not to sell everything, but to tighten your stop losses, reduce position sizes slightly, and make sure your portfolio can survive a sudden 5–10% market correction without you needing to make panic decisions.
